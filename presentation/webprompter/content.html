    • First off, we’ll clarify something so you don’t get too confused. The word “Mamluk” literally means “owned” and can refer to two things: the slave, or the dynasty.

  • Slide 3
    • The empire was formed in the year 1250 and lasted until 1517 as an independent empire, a time span of 267 years.
  • Slide 4
    • The Mamluks ruled an area over a part of Africa and Asia (Syria). Their capital was located in Cairo, Egypt. If you travel to Egypt, parts of the Mamluk dynasty still remain.
  • Slide 5
    • The Mamluks were Turkish slaves who were the soldiers in the military of the Abbasid Caliphate. Caliph al-Mu’tasim used these Mamluks to boost the strength of his army.
    • After his demise, Turkish Mamluks slowly rebelled by killing or deposing caliphs.
    • The caliphate lost power and became merely a symbol of authority. Mamluk generals held the real power.
    • Dynasties formed consisted of sultans who were originally Mamluks or descendants of these slaves.
    • They overthrew the caliph and claimed control of an area.
    • By the 13th century, they had established their presence in Egypt and India.
    • Aybak was the first Mamluk sultan, married the wife of the last Ayyubid Caliph, and ruled from 1250 to 1257.
    • The first leaders after him were elite Turkish or Mongol slave officers.
  • Slide 6
    • The Mamluks government was basically structured as an oligarchy and was based on a military fief system. Slaves were the lowest class, superseded by a small middle class, then nobles, with the sultan having the most power.
  • Slide 7
    • The system of succession was not hereditary - unlike in lots of other governments where sons would follow their fathers. In Mamluk society, this was the exception rather than the rule.
    • In the early ages of the Mamluk empire, sons often followed their fathers, but after 1390 this changed into gaining power through force. In fact, slaves did not even have the right to claim someone as part of their family, so they had no way to prove a hereditary relationship.
    • This caused bloody battles between prospective sultans. The system was therefore inefficient and very violent.
    • The sultan successor was chosen based on prior military experience and rose up the chain of command through promotions.
    • The government was controlled by the military rather than being separate units. Soldiers directed the administration and monitored civilian officials.

    • The Mamuks had very advanced studies in astronomy. Astronomy was an important field in the Mamluk empire, as in all of Islam.
  • Slide 22
    • Centers of astronomy in Baghdad, Iran, Syria, and Egypt have moved over time to reflect the varying concentrations of scholars.
    • Mamluk astronomical knowledge built upon the five centuries of Islamic astronomy that preceded it.
  • Slide 23
    • The center of astronomy in the Mamluk empire moved over time. Cairo and Damascus had both been important cities for some time, but when two astronomers, Mu’ayyad al-Din al-’Urdi and Muhily al-Din al-Maghribi, were transferred to an observatory in Cairo, Cairo became more important.
    • The provinical capital was moved to Damascus in the mid 14th century, and the center followed the Syrain astronomers that went to Damascus.
    • But when Timur raided Damasus in 1401, the center went back to Cairo. Some of the leading astronomers there were associated with the Azhar mosque.
    • It remained there until the end of the 16th century when it was moved to Istanbul.
  • Slide 24
    • Most of what we know about the Mamluk’s astronomy comes from manuscripts and instruments.
  • Slide 25
    • Mamluks extensively studied astronomy for astrology, navigation, and timekeeping.
    • They divided astronomy into five fields: theoretical, mathematical, spherical, science of instruments, and astrology.
    • Theoretical astronomy, or ‘ilm al-hav’a, studied geometric models of the movements of the sun and planets.
    • Mathematical astronomy, or ‘ilm al-zijat, used these models to determine positions. These were recorded in tables in zijs, of which over 200 are known.
    • Spherical astronomy, another branch of ‘ilm al-zijat, studied the apparent movement of the stars. This had a practical application: ‘ilm al-migat, timekeeping using the positions of the stars. The only way to keep time was to read and analyze the sun’s position in the sky. The same was used to study the stars.
    • The science of instruments, or ‘ilm al-alat, created instruments for representing this apparent movement, including the celestial sphere, astrolabe, and the quadrant. The quadrant was made to be better than the astrolabe.
    • Astrology, or ‘ilm ahkam al-nujam, or ‘ilm al-tanjim, was widely practiced in the empire. Unfortunately, few material remains.

  • Slide 29
    • Ultimately, the empire came to a close.
    • Several factors contributed to their demise.
    • The reason for most declines are the unstable governments. As policing is reduced, corruption runs rampant and the dynasty or empire will fall apart. The Cricassian government system caused constant struggle for the throne.
    • The Black Death, occurring from 1347-1349, was a plague that wiped out 1/3 of the Egyptian population. It was one of the worst plagues in Europe’s history, so it really took a toll on the Mamluk civilization.  
    • As a result of this reduced population, agricultural production declined, causing incomes to fall. This lowered the amount of money paid to the sultans, so taxes went up.
    • The Mamluks had once enjoyed a dominance in trade in Mediterranean Europe. Competition from newly opened trade routes and the new Portuguese spice trade competition were detriments to their trading industry.
    • Constant Mongol and Ottoman struggles created tension in the empire.
  • Slide 30
    • After many failed attempts, the Ottomans defeated the Mamluks in the year 1517.
      • While the Mamluks still lived on as a tribal group, the sultanate was destroyed and Egypt and India became provinces of the Ottoman Empire.
      • Beys rose to power as the leaders of these provinces, staying in power until the French occupation in 1798 ended their rule.
    • The empire was finally defeated in 1811 by Muhammad Ali.
      • Wanting to eliminate his only threat, he invited the Mamluk leaders to a celebration at the Cairo Citadel.
      • Then, as they arrived, he slaughtered them all. He then sent out his army throughout the empire to take care of the remainder of the military.
      • This massacre ended the Mamluks’ empire and regime.
